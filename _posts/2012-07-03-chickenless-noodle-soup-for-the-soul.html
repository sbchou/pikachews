On Sunday I woke up and inspired by my roommate who was cleaning the room, full nesting mode was on. After a quick trip to the gym (going to the gym for Sophie is a rare occurrence and has never persisted for more than half an hour. I much prefer yoga and dancing--the treadmill is just so boring!), I went to Whole Foods, leaving triumphant with an armful's worth of veggies and other feel-good foods. Plus two pretty shades of summery polish from Walgreens for just $2.99 each!

Kabocha squash  is definitely one of my favorite veggies (and I think I may have converted my suitemate, too!). Any way you make it, it's an extremely dense, creamy, and sweet squash -- nothing like the old Jack'O Lantern. Since I had a bit of time, I decided to compare two cooking methods: simmered sweet & savory on the stovetop, and roasted (along with the seeds!) sprinkled with cinnamon.

For cooking method Number One, simply chop up the pumpkin into nice chunks, cover with about 3/4 cup of water, a tablespoon of soy sauce, and a dash of sugar. Bring to a boil, then lower heat and simmer until all liquid is gone.

The key here is patience! About half an hour, so that the pumpkin is really soft and tender. So delicious! The whole thing melts in the mouth--skin, too.

For Part Two, I amped the oven on to 400F and, again, chopped up the squash, coating with a thin layer of olive oil (same for seeds). After spreading on a baking sheet, I sprinkled the squash with a touch of cinnamon, and the seeds with some cumin. Then, I let it go for at least 20 minutes, or until browned and fork-tender.

The verdict? Both were fantastic, but I preferred the stewing method (a bit more traditional/Asian taste).  Since the squash is already very dry and sweet, roasting made it dry out even more.

In elementary school my kindergarten teacher would dress up as a pumpkin every year and tell her students that it was because she ate too much pumpkin. I'm starting to feel like a pumpkin myself right about now, because this kabocha recipe is just so damn addictive. I ate half the whole thing for dinner yesterday, and the other half today in any easy  noodle soup: leftover udon, frozen mixed veggies, and yesterday's pumpkin boiled in some water, plus one raw egg beaten into the broth for egg drop.

A comforting dinner & the perfect salve for Mondays!
